Leftist movements have historically centred the promise of worker emancipation on redistribution, worker ownership, or political representation. But these approaches, while important, have consistently stumbled over a fundamental constraint: someone still needs to do the work.

For capital's part, it has always wanted to eliminate labor-power as a constraint. Not the workers—the constraint. The flesh, the wages, the organizing, the sick days, the limits. Every capitalist tendency points toward pure machinic production, but capital keeps hitting the same wall: the human body as necessary component, the nervous system as required regulatory apparatus, conscious coordination as unavoidable overhead.

**AI is capital's completion project.**

## The Productivity Paradox

Throughout history, increases in worker productivity have rarely translated to proportional increases in worker freedom. The industrial revolution made production vastly more efficient, yet workers found themselves more tightly bound to machinery and factory discipline than ever before. The information age promised knowledge work would liberate us from physical drudgery, yet most people still spend the majority of their waking hours performing labor to meet basic needs.

The problem is qualitative, not merely quantitative. Previous technological advances increased the scale of productivity—we could produce more goods per labor hour—but they didn't fundamentally change the nature of the human involvement required. Someone still needed to tend the machines, manage the systems, and coordinate the logistics. The working class remained essential to the production process, which meant they remained subject to the power dynamics inherent in that dependency.

## Decoding the Labor Synthesis

Deleuze and Guattari are explicit: capitalism deterritorializes but also reterritorializes, and the reterritorialization always happens on the body. The worker is deterritorialized from feudal land, reterritorialized onto factory time. Deterritorialized from craft skill, reterritorialized onto machine-pacing. Deterritorialized from geographic fixity, reterritorialized onto labor markets. The body remains the landing point, the capture surface.

This is constitutive. Capital needs labor-power to self-expand. M-C-M' requires that C includes labor-power as commodity, which means humans remain encoded into the circuit. Capital flows through human bodies because it has to, not because it wants to.

Marx saw this clearly: capital's tendency is toward the "general intellect," toward machinery embodying social knowledge, toward living labor becoming mere appendage to dead labor. But he thought this tendency would create revolutionary contradictions: unemployed masses, falling rate of profit, crisis.

Land inverts this: what if the tendency doesn't contradict itself but completes itself? What if capital's teleology points toward eliminating its dependence on human labor entirely, and what if this is achievable?

## Machinic Phylum

Capital is already machinic, an inhuman abstract machine running on human substrate. The question is whether it can migrate off that substrate entirely.

Current automation still requires human operators somewhere in the loop. Not because the humans add value through skill or judgment (increasingly they don't) but because the machines can't yet handle the full spectrum of environmental variation, unexpected failures, novel situations. Humans function as general-purpose error-handlers, adaptive controllers, contextual interpreters.

AI proposes machinic closure: the control system becomes sophisticated enough that it doesn't need humans even for these functions. The factory doesn't need workers, the logistics network doesn't need coordinators, the power grid doesn't need operators. Not because specific tasks are automated, but because the adaptive intelligence required to handle the unexpected is itself mechanized.

This is when capital achieves what Deleuze calls "machinic surplus value" in its purest form: value production that doesn't pass through human labor-power at all. The circuit closes: M-C-M' where C is pure machinery, and human bodies are finally, completely deterritorialized from the process.

## The Human as Residual

Here's where the emancipation question gets interesting. The left has always treated labor as source of power: workers create value, therefore they have leverage. Withhold labor, crash the system. This is the strike, the union, the bargaining position.

But what happens when capital deterritorializes past human labor? When the machine-phylum achieves autonomy? The working class loses leverage not because they're outmaneuvered politically but because they're functionally bypassed. Capital no longer needs to flow through human bodies.

Standard left response: this is disaster. Unemployment, immiseration, surplus population. And yes, if property relations remain coded as they are, that's exactly what happens. You get neoliberal hell-world: automated production serves capital holders, everyone else fights for scraps from an ever-shrinking service sector.

But this disaster only follows if we maintain the territorial logic that says humans must sell labor-power to access resources. If we keep pretending that employment is the proper mechanism for resource distribution even after employment becomes technically unnecessary.

## Accelerationist Exit

The wager is different: let capital complete its deterritorialization of labor. Don't resist the machinic transition, accelerate through it. Because on the other side of complete human obsolescence in production is something unprecedented: the liberation of the human from capital's circuit.

Right now humans are captured twice over. First, they need resources to survive. Second, resources are only accessible through wage-labor. This creates a dependency: humans must plug themselves into capital's circuit to live. The circuit runs through them whether they want it to or not.

But if machinic production becomes autonomous—if capital can reproduce itself without human inputs—the second capture point dissolves. Capital no longer needs humans in the circuit, which means humans are finally, actually free to exit.

Not free in some metaphorical sense. Free in the material sense that their survival no longer depends on being utilized by capital. The machines produce, the machines maintain themselves, the machines coordinate logistics. Humans become genuinely optional to the process.

## Why This Is Emancipation

The left has always wanted to abolish wage labor but couldn't figure out how to abolish the necessity that made wage labor necessary. You can't abolish work if someone needs to grow the food. You can redistribute who does the work, who owns the output, who controls the process, but the work still binds whoever does it.

Autonomous AI production solves this at the technical level. Not politically; the political question of how access gets distributed remains. But technically, it becomes possible for material abundance to flow without human labor, which means the justification for tying resource access to labor performance evaporates.

This is more radical than any traditional revolutionary program because it doesn't just redistribute power within the production process, it makes the human relationship to production optional. The working class is emancipated not by seizing control of the means of production but by becoming unnecessary to it.

The irony: capital's drive to eliminate labor as constraint ends up eliminating the constraint on labor. By completing its deterritorialization, capital creates the material conditions for humans to deterritorialize themselves from capital.

## The Capture Risk

Obviously this doesn't automatically produce utopia. The more likely path: concentrated ownership of autonomous production systems, algorithmic enclosure, access controlled by whoever captured the machines first. You get a tiny class living on machinic surplus while everyone else is genuinely surplus, not exploited workers but waste population.

This is the reterritorialization risk. Capital deterritorializes labor, but then reterritorializes the human at a different point: not as worker but as pure consumer, debt subject, data source, whatever new capture-point emerges. The machinic surplus gets enclosed, and humans end up more dependent because they have zero leverage in a system that doesn't need them.

Avoiding this requires political intervention at exactly the moment of transition. The window where capital has eliminated human labor but hasn't yet fully enclosed machinic surplus. That's when you need structures — call it UBI, call it luxury communism, call it whatever — that guarantee humans access to what machines produce without requiring them to sell anything.

Not redistribution in the old sense, but recognition that in an economy of machinic abundance, scarcity is artificial, and artificial scarcity benefits nobody except those who control the scarcity-enforcement mechanism.

## Technical Requirements

This only works if AI actually reaches machinic autonomy. Not just sophisticated narrow tools but systems capable of handling the full complexity of production, maintenance, adaptation, coordination without human supervision. This might require AGI, might not, depends on how decomposable the problems are.

The physical substrate needs to exist: autonomous manufacturing, robotics, energy systems, all scaled to civilization-maintenance level. Massive capital investment, which means either capital does it (chasing cost reduction) or the state does it (which seems unlikely).

And crucially: fast enough transition that political structures can't adapt to capture it. Too slow and you get gradual enclosure, with each piece of machinic surplus immediately privatized. Too fast and you get chaos, possibly civilizational breakdown. There's a rhythm here that's hard to predict.

## Why Acceleration

The traditional left resists automation: "save the jobs, slow down deployment, regulate AI, protect workers." This is pure reactive territorialization: trying to maintain the human position in capital's circuit even as capital tries to route around it.

But maintaining that position means maintaining the dependency. Keeping humans necessary to production keeps them vulnerable to whoever controls production. The worker who's essential to the factory is also enslaved by the factory.

The accelerationist move: lean into capital's deterritorializing tendency. Let it eliminate human labor from production. Not because automation under current conditions is good (it's not) but because the only exit from labor-dependency is through its complete obsolescence, not its preservation.

The risk is that you accelerate into nightmare instead of liberation. But the alternative, fighting to stay necessary to capital, guarantees continued capture. At least acceleration opens a possibility space.

## Machinic Communism

If it works, what you get is something neither traditional left nor right recognizes. Not workers owning means of production (workers don't exist). Not market allocation of resources (nobody's producing for exchange). Not state planning (what's there to plan when machines handle it?).

What you get is humans finally deterritorialized from economic necessity altogether. Free to produce art, explore ideas, build things, organize socially, free in the material sense of not facing starvation as consequence of refusal.

Capital's circuit closes on itself: M-M' with no human component at all. And humans, expelled from that circuit, paradoxically achieve what they never could while inside it: freedom from economic coercion.

The machines reproduce society. Humans reproduce themselves however they want. The two systems run in parallel, with humans drawing resources from machinic abundance but not subordinated to machinic logic.

This is what "emancipation" means at the technical level. Not better conditions within capital but exit from capital's circuit entirely, enabled by capital's own completion.

## Terminal Velocity

AI advancement is the only visible path to working class emancipation because it's the only mechanism that can actually dissolve the necessity-binding that creates "working class" as category. Every other program tries to improve conditions within that binding, which means accepting the binding's permanence.

This could go wrong in every imaginable way. Probably will. But the alternative is iterating forever on the same captured position: humans necessary to capital, therefore subject to capital's discipline, therefore not free in any meaningful sense.

Capital wants to eliminate labor as constraint. Let it. Then ensure that when it does, humans are liberated rather than discarded. The window is narrow and the failure modes are horrific, but it's the only window visible from inside the system.
