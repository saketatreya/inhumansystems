# Hindutva and the Production of Hyperreal Hinduism: A Genealogy of Self-Propagating Mythology

## I. The Genealogical Problem: What Requires Explanation

In 1980, the Bharatiya Janata Party won 2 seats in the Lok Sabha. In 2019, it won 303. This is not simply electoral success but a transformation in what can be politically articulated, what can be publicly desired, what forms of violence become acceptable. The question is not whether Hindutva ideology is "true" or "false" but how it produces its own truth-effects, how it makes itself real through specific practices, institutions, and circulations of affect and violence.

The conventional explanation, that the BJP mobilized latent Hindu majoritarian sentiment explains nothing. It merely displaces the question: where does this "sentiment" come from? How does it become politically operative? What transformations in subjectivity, in the organization of social space, in the structure of desire, make Hindutva not just possible but compelling for millions?

In this essay I argue that we must understand Hindutva as a productive apparatus that generates mythology, and that this mythology is self-propagating not because it corresponds to reality but because it creates feedback loops connecting institutional practice, affective investment, territorial violence, and electoral calculation. The apparatus operates across multiple registers simultaneously; the daily ritual of the shakha, the architectural destruction of mosques, the circulation of WhatsApp videos, the reorganization of textbooks, the administrative production of citizenship, etc, and these disparate practices reinforce each other in ways that make the system resilient to empirical refutation.

But this apparatus contains a foundational contradiction that both enables and threatens its operation: the attempt to produce unified "Hindu" identity from a social formation (the caste system) that operates precisely through internal differentiation, hierarchy, and mutual unintelligibility across caste boundaries. This is not an accidental tension but a constitutive aporia that generates the perpetual motion of Hindutva mythology, its need for constant production of new enemies, new crises, new mobilizations to paper over the internal fractures that would otherwise be visible.

## II. The Colonial Archive and the Production of Hinduism-as-Object

### The Census as Productive Technology

Any genealogy must begin not with what Hinduism "is" but with the specific practices through which "Hinduism" became thinkable as a unified object. The most crucial of these was the colonial census, which between 1871 and 1931 progressively hardened religious categories and demanded that Indian subjects identify themselves within these categories.

Consider the epistemic violence this entailed. A Brahmin Shaivite from Tamil Nadu who followed Advaita Vedanta, a Vaishnava weaver from Gujarat who worshipped Vitthal, a Shakta tribal woman from Bengal who practiced animal sacrifice, and an untouchable leatherworker from UP who had his own village deity; these people shared almost nothing in terms of practice, belief, philosophical framework, ritual life, or social position. The term "Hindu" would have meant nothing to most of them as a descriptor of their primary identity.

The census didn't discover that these people were all "Hindus"; it produced them as such. More precisely, it created the administrative and epistemological conditions under which "Hindu" could become a meaningful identity category. The census operation required:

- **Territorial boundedness**: Religious identity tied to presence on Indian soil, producing "Hinduism" as indigenous versus Islam and Christianity as foreign
- **Mutual exclusivity**: Each person must be one religion only, erasing practices of multiple belonging and syncretism
- **Enumerability**: Religions must be countable populations that could be compared, generating the anxiety about relative numbers that persists today
- **Textual reification**: Census officers determined who was "Hindu" partly by reference to Sanskrit texts, elevating Brahminical textual tradition as definitional

This produced what Bernard Cohn called the "investigative modalities" of colonial rule—ways of knowing that simultaneously created their objects. Indians didn't simply respond to census categories; they began organizing politically around them. The Arya Samaj (1875), the Theosophical Society (1875), and the Hindu Mahasabha (1915) all participated in constructing "Hinduism" as a bounded, unified tradition capable of being defended, reformed, and mobilized.

But note the class and caste composition of this construction: it was an overwhelmingly upper-caste, urban, educated elite project. The Arya Samaj, despite its critique of Brahminical ritual, was dominated by urban Punjabi Khatris and Brahmins. Its vision of "Vedic Hinduism" had nothing to do with how most Indians actually practiced religion. What was being constructed was not a representation of existing Hinduism but a new object—reformed, rationalized, unified—modeled on Protestant Christianity's emphasis on scripture, belief, and congregational worship.

### The Production of Tradition Through Antagonism

The construction of Hinduism-as-object occurred dialectically through antagonism with Islam and Christianity. This is crucial: the unity of "Hinduism" was not discovered internally but produced externally through defining what Hinduism was not.

Swami Vivekananda's 1893 Chicago speech is instructive. He presented "Hinduism" to Western audiences as a universal, tolerant, philosophical spirituality—everything Christianity supposedly was not. But this presentation bore little resemblance to the reality of caste-divided, ritual-diverse Indian religious practice. Vivekananda was not describing Hinduism; he was producing a new object called "Hinduism" that could function in transnational circulation as a civilizational identity.

Similarly, the cow protection movements of the 1880s-90s didn't simply defend existing practice (many lower-caste Hindus ate beef; the Vedic Aryans sacrificed cattle). Rather, they produced cow worship as the marker of Hindu identity precisely through antagonism with Muslim cattle slaughter. The cow became a boundary object—marking where Hindu ended and Muslim began—and this boundary production was more important than any actual theological or scriptural basis.

This is the first mechanism of mythological self-propagation: identity is produced through antagonism, and the antagonism then appears to confirm the preexisting identity it actually created. Muslims slaughter cows → this offends Hindus → therefore Hindus have always worshipped cows → therefore Muslims are enemies of Hindu tradition. The circuit erases its own historical production.

## III. The RSS: Engineering Fascist Subjectivity in Postcolonial Space

### The Shakha as Technology of the Self

The RSS, founded in Nagpur in 1925 by Keshav Baliram Hedgewar, represents a radical discontinuity in Hindu nationalist practice. Its innovation was not ideological but technical: the shakha as a daily practice of subject formation.

The shakha operates through the precise choreography of bodies, space, and time:

- **Spatial organization**: Participants arrange themselves in rows facing the bhagwa dhwaj (saffron flag), creating a visual field oriented toward a sacred center. The flag itself is worshipped at the beginning of each session—this is not metaphorical but literal worship, with all the affective investment that entails. The sacred object is not a deity but a symbol of the organization itself.
- **Temporal rhythm**: The shakha meets daily at the same time (typically early morning or evening), creating bodily habituation. This is not occasional attendance but daily practice that structures time itself around RSS belonging. Over months and years, this produces a deep corporeal memory—the body knows the rhythm of the shakha.
- **Physical drill**: Exercises are performed in unison—surya namaskar, danda (stick drill), games. This synchronized movement produces what Émile Durkheim called "collective effervescence"—the heightened affective state that comes from moving as one body. But it also inscribes discipline: learning to move when commanded, to synchronize with others, to subordinate individual will to collective rhythm.
- **Ideological instruction**: After physical training, participants sit for baudhik (intellectual session) where a senior pracharak (instructor) delivers a lesson. These lessons are highly formulaic, repeating basic themes: Hindu civilization is 5000+ years old, Hindus faced 1000 years of Muslim oppression, Hindus must unite to reclaim their glory. The content is less important than the repetition—ideology entering through rhythm rather than argument.
- **Prarthana (prayer)**: The session ends with collective prayer to Bharat Mata and the singing of RSS songs. This produces affective closure—the sense of completion, of having participated in something meaningful.

Now, what does this produce? Not simply ideological conviction but a habituated subject who has literally incorporated RSS ideology at the level of bodily practice. Foucault distinguished between technologies of domination (power over others) and technologies of the self (practices through which individuals constitute themselves as subjects). The shakha is primarily the latter—participants actively produce themselves as Hindu nationalist subjects through daily practice.

This is crucial for understanding self-propagation. The ideology is not imposed externally but produced internally through practice. A shakha member doesn't just believe in Hindu unity; he has performed it daily through synchronized exercise. He hasn't just learned about Muslim threat; he has oriented his body toward the saffron flag and away from imagined enemies. The mythology is inscribed at a pre-discursive level—in habit, affect, corporeal memory.

Moreover, the shakha produces horizontal bonds among participants—friendships, mutual aid, networks of trust. These bonds then function to reinforce ideology. Leaving the RSS means not just changing beliefs but severing social ties, abandoning community, losing a structure of daily meaning. The cost of exit rises with each year of participation.

### The Organizational Form: Cellular Proliferation Without Center

The RSS's organizational structure reveals a profound understanding of how power operates in decentralized networks. Unlike a hierarchical party with central command, the RSS operates through semi-autonomous cells that reproduce themselves.

Each shakha is run by local pracharaks who have undergone training at RSS camps but operate with significant autonomy in their locality. The shakha adapts to local conditions—different regions emphasize different issues, use different languages, mobilize around different antagonisms. This flexibility allows the organization to penetrate diverse contexts while maintaining ideological consistency through ritual practice.

The training of pracharaks occurs at special camps where men (the RSS is male-only, with parallel organizations for women and children) spend weeks or months in total immersion. This is not education but transformation—the production of cadre whose entire subjectivity is organized around RSS mission. These pracharaks then return to establish new shakhas, which train new pracharaks, creating exponential growth.

By the 1940s, the RSS had expanded to approximately 600,000 members across India. But membership numbers understate influence. The shakha produces not just members but a particular kind of subject—disciplined, ideologically committed, willing to mobilize for action. These subjects then occupy positions in civil society—as teachers, journalists, bureaucrats, police officers—extending RSS influence far beyond formal membership.

The genius of this cellular form is its resilience. When the RSS was banned after Gandhi's assassination in 1948, individual shakhas continued meeting informally. When the ban lifted in 1949, the organization reconstituted rapidly because the cellular structure had survived. There was no central headquarters to destroy, no leader whose removal would collapse the organization. This is genuinely rhizomatic—multiple entry points, multiple connections, no single root.

### Golwalkar and the Theorization of Organic Unity

M.S. Golwalkar, who led the RSS from 1940-1973, provided the ideological systematization. His 1939 text "We, or Our Nationhood Defined" and 1966 collection "Bunch of Thoughts" articulate with startling explicitness what Hindu rashtra means.

Golwalkar's key move is biologizing the nation. He writes: "A Nation is a group of human beings who have a common race, common religion, common language, and common culture...all the four must be there." This is not civic nationalism (shared citizenship) or even cultural nationalism (shared values) but racial nationalism. The nation is an organic body, and minorities are foreign elements within this body.

The implications are stated clearly: "Germany has also shown how well-nigh impossible it is for Races and cultures, having differences going to the root, to be assimilated into one united whole, a good lesson for us in Hindusthan to learn and profit by." This was written in 1939, as Nazi Germany was implementing its racial laws. Golwalkar is explicit: India should learn from Nazi treatment of Jews how to handle Muslims.

The organic metaphor does crucial ideological work. If the nation is a body, then:
- Muslims and Christians are foreign organs, creating "disease"
- Hindu unity is natural health of the body
- Violence against minorities is immune response, not aggression
- Caste is natural differentiation within the body (like organs serving different functions)

This last point is how Golwalkar attempts to resolve the caste contradiction. He writes: "In our conception of what the Hindu nation is, we have taken into account the need for every individual and group to have their identity...there is no place for the concept of equality." Caste hierarchy is retheorized as functional differentiation within organic unity—like the brain, hands, and feet of a body, all necessary but not equal.

But this analogy reveals the aporia. Brains, hands, and feet don't develop separate consciousness, don't have antagonistic interests, don't need to be mobilized into unity. Castes do. The organic metaphor papers over the fundamental reality: castes are not complementary organs but competing groups with irreconcilable interests rooted in material exploitation and ritual degradation. The metaphor is not description but production—an attempt to create what it claims to represent.

## IV. Caste as Permanent Crisis: The Foundational Contradiction

### The Sociological Reality of Caste Division

To understand why Hindutva mythology must be self-propagating, we must grasp the sociological reality it confronts. Caste is not simply hierarchy but a comprehensive system of segregation touching every aspect of life:

- **Endogamy**: Marriage within caste is enforced through violence. Inter-caste marriages, especially Dalit-savarna marriages, regularly result in honor killings. The National Crime Records Bureau reports hundreds of such murders annually, though actual numbers are certainly higher. Endogamy means that caste reproduces biologically across generations.
- **Occupational specialization**: Despite legal prohibition, many Dalits remain trapped in stigmatized occupations—manual scavenging (cleaning human waste), leatherwork, sanitation. These occupations are not just economically marginal but ritually polluting, placing those who perform them outside normal social interaction.
- **Spatial segregation**: In villages, Dalit bastis (settlements) are physically separate from caste Hindu areas. Dalits cannot draw water from common wells, cannot enter temples, cannot eat in restaurants. In cities, segregation continues through ghettoization—Dalit neighborhoods are relegated to peripheries, denied infrastructure and services.
- **Violence**: Caste violence is systematic, not exceptional. The data from National Crime Records Bureau shows that every 15 minutes, a crime against a Dalit occurs; every day, six Dalit women are raped. But these statistics undercount because most violence goes unreported due to police indifference or complicity.
- **Economic exploitation**: Dalits remain disproportionately poor, landless, and dependent on caste Hindu landlords for employment. This economic subordination reinforces social hierarchy—challenging caste means risking livelihood.

This is the social reality that Hindutva must somehow transform into unified "Hindu" identity. The gulf between a Brahmin and a Bhangi (traditional sweeper caste) is not merely cultural difference but comprehensive separation enforced through violence. They do not intermarry, eat together, worship together, or recognize shared identity. How does such radical division become unity?

### The RSS's Caste Composition: Brahminical Hegemony

Examining the caste composition of RSS leadership reveals the organization's actual social base, which contradicts its rhetoric of Hindu unity.

RSS Sarsanghchalaks (supreme leaders):
- K.B. Hedgewar (1925-1940): Brahmin
- M.S. Golwalkar (1940-1973): Brahmin
- Madhukar Dattatraya Deoras (1973-1994): Brahmin
- Rajendra Singh (1994-2000): Rajput (upper caste)
- K.S. Sudarshan (2000-2009): Brahmin
- Mohan Bhagwat (2009-present): Brahmin

Key ideologues:
- V.D. Savarkar: Chitpavan Brahmin
- Deendayal Upadhyaya: Brahmin

All the major theorists are upper caste. Data from studies of RSS membership show that while the organization has expanded into OBC communities, Brahmins and other upper castes remain vastly overrepresented in leadership positions at every level. A Dalit pracharak is exceptional; a Dalit sarsanghchalak is unimaginable.

Moreover, the RSS's vision of "Hindu culture" is recognizably Brahminical:
- Vegetarianism is promoted as Hindu virtue (erasing lower-caste meat-eating traditions)
- Sanskrit is valorized as sacred language (erasing vernacular traditions)
- Brahmanical festivals and practices are universalized as Hindu
- Dalit-Bahujan practices (folk traditions, non-Brahmanical deities, beef-eating) are either erased or condemned as un-Hindu

This is not accidental but structural. The RSS was founded by upper-caste men concerned about the erosion of their social position under colonialism and modernization. Its function has always been to preserve and extend upper-caste hegemony while mobilizing lower castes as foot soldiers against Muslims.

### The Mandal Commission Crisis: Caste Consciousness Versus Hindu Unity

The 1990 announcement of Mandal Commission implementation crystallized the contradiction. The commission recommended 27% reservation in government jobs for OBCs (in addition to existing 22.5% for SC/ST). This threatened to redistribute resources from upper castes to OBCs and made caste identity politically salient.

The upper-caste response was immediate and violent. Students, primarily from upper castes, launched protests. Several engaged in self-immolation. The rhetoric was telling: reservations were "reverse discrimination," "casteism," "division of Hindu society." The irony was palpable—upper castes, who had maintained brutal caste hierarchy for millennia, now discovered the language of equality and merit when their monopoly was threatened.

The BJP was caught in a bind. Its upper-caste base was furious about reservations. But OBCs constituted 40%+ of the electorate. Supporting Mandal would alienate the base; opposing it would prevent OBC mobilization necessary for electoral success.

L.K. Advani's Rath Yatra was the solution. In September-October 1990, Advani traveled from Somnath (Gujarat) to Ayodhya (UP) in a motorized "chariot" (actually a Toyota van decorated to look like a mythological chariot), demanding the destruction of Babri Masjid and construction of a Ram temple.

The journey was designed to perform several functions simultaneously:
- **Spatial appropriation**: The route traversed multiple states, territorially marking India as Hindu space through procession
- **Mythological invocation**: The chariot evoked images of Krishna and Arjuna from the Mahabharata, collapsing historical time into mythological time
- **Communal mobilization**: At each stop, riots were choreographed, creating Hindu-Muslim violence that then justified calls for Hindu unity
- **Caste subsumption**: By making Muslim antagonism the primary political question, caste conflict could be temporarily suppressed

The violence was extraordinary. Advani's journey left a trail of communal riots. Approximately 200 people were killed during the Rath Yatra itself. But this violence was not failure; it was the point. Each riot created polarization, and polarization created conditions for BJP electoral success.

The logic is cynical but effective: create communal violence → Hindus fear Muslims → caste differences become secondary → vote for BJP to protect Hindu interests. This is Mandir (temple) overriding Mandal (caste reservations).

### The Functional Deployment of OBCs

The BJP's relationship with OBCs reveals the mechanics of mythological capture. The party needed OBC votes but couldn't afford genuine OBC empowerment, which would threaten upper-caste dominance.

The solution was symbolic representation combined with structural subordination. OBC leaders were promoted to visible positions—chief ministers, cabinet ministers, party presidents—but actual power remained with upper-caste RSS leadership.

Consider Narendra Modi. He is promoted as an OBC (Ghanchi-Teli) leader who rose from tea-selling to prime minister, embodying meritocracy and social mobility. But Modi's actual caste identity is complex and contested. Ghanchis are an OBC caste, but Modi's family's economic position and social capital were not typical of marginalized OBCs. Moreover, Modi has no record of fighting for OBC rights or challenging upper-caste dominance. His OBC identity is performative—deployed for electoral appeal while his governance serves upper-caste and corporate interests.

This pattern repeats. Kalyan Singh, Dalit chief minister of UP (1991-1992), presided over the destruction of Babri Masjid, weaponizing his Dalit identity to legitimate upper-caste violence. Uma Bharti, another OBC leader, was celebrated when useful for mobilization, sidelined when she threatened actual power.

The mythology absorbs OBC aspiration by offering individual mobility (you too could become chief minister!) while maintaining collective subordination (OBC communities remain economically and socially marginalized). This is neoliberal meritocracy—a few tokens rise while structural hierarchy persists.

### Dalit Resistance and Attempted Capture

Dalits have historically been most resistant to Hindutva because they have most to lose from Hindu unity rhetoric. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar recognized early that "Hindu" unity meant Brahminical domination. He converted to Buddhism and urged Dalits to leave Hinduism entirely, recognizing that the religion was inseparable from caste oppression.

The RSS response to Dalit resistance has evolved through three strategies:
1. **Integration through Sanskritization**: Encouraging Dalits to adopt upper-caste practices (vegetarianism, Sanskrit names, ritual purity) as path to social mobility. This keeps Dalits within "Hindu" fold while reinforcing Brahminical cultural dominance.
2. **Communal Mobilization**: Recruiting Dalits into anti-Muslim violence by offering temporary sense of power and superiority over Muslims. Gujarat 2002 saw significant Dalit participation in anti-Muslim riots, weaponizing Dalit resentment for upper-caste political goals.
3. **Violent Suppression**: When Dalits assert autonomous political identity or challenge upper-caste dominance, the response is violence. The 2016 flogging of Dalit youth in Una (Gujarat) for skinning dead cattle, the 2020 Hathras gang-rape and forced cremation, the regular atrocities against Dalits by cow protection vigilantes—all demonstrate that Hindu unity rhetoric evaporates when Dalits claim actual equality.

These strategies reveal the contradiction at work. Hindutva needs Dalit numbers for electoral arithmetic but cannot tolerate Dalit autonomy. The mythology must therefore constantly produce Dalits as subordinate-but-included—part of Hindu fold but knowing their place.

## V. The Ram Janmabhoomi Movement: Producing Sacred Territory Through Violence

### Archaeological Uncertainty and Mythological Certainty

The Babri Masjid controversy revolves around an epistemological claim: that the mosque was built after destroying a temple marking Ram's exact birthplace. This claim confronts immediate problems:
- **Ram is mythological**: The Ramayana is an epic poem, not historical chronicle. No credible historian treats Ram as a historical figure whose birthdate and birthplace can be archaeologically verified.
- **Archaeological evidence is ambiguous**: Excavations show temple remains beneath the mosque, but dating them is complex, proving they specifically marked Ram's birthplace is impossible, and proving the mosque was built through destruction rather than repurposing existing materials is contested.
- **The claim is recent**: The specific assertion that this site is Ram's exact birthplace dates to the 19th century. Prior to colonialism, there's little evidence of this being a major pilgrimage site or contentious location.

But Hindutva ideology doesn't engage these empirical questions. Instead, it asserts that faith supersedes evidence, that Hindu belief about Ram's birthplace IS the relevant truth regardless of archaeology or history.

This is hyperreality in its precise Baudrillardian sense. The simulation (mythological birthplace) doesn't correspond to reality but replaces reality as what is real. The VHP's rhetoric is telling: "Mandir wahin banayenge" (We will build the temple right there). Not "we believe" or "we hope" but "we will"—assertion of will over fact, of faith over evidence, of political power over epistemic humility.

This mechanism is crucial for understanding self-propagation. By asserting that mythology is superior truth, the movement becomes unfalsifiable. Archaeological evidence can't refute the claim because archaeology is positioned as inferior to faith. Historians can't challenge the narrative because "secular historians" are positioned as biased against Hindus. The mythology has created its own epistemology in which it is automatically true.

### The Mobilization Infrastructure: From Myth to Movement

The transformation of a mythological claim into mass mobilization required extensive organizational infrastructure. The VHP's campaign from 1984 onward operated through multiple techniques:

- **Shila pujan (brick worship)**: The VHP organized ceremonies across India where bricks were consecrated to be sent to Ayodhya for temple construction. This achieved several effects: (1) Distributed participation—millions of Hindus across India could "participate" in temple building by donating bricks, creating affective investment. (2) Ritual intensification—the bricks weren't ordinary construction materials but sacred objects, making the campaign religious rather than merely political. (3) Nationalization of local space—even in distant villages, the ceremony oriented participants toward Ayodhya, producing India as sacred geography oriented toward this center.
- **Rath Yatra**: Advani's 1990 chariot procession was theatrical politics—producing spectacle that generated media coverage while marking territory through movement. The route was carefully chosen to traverse UP and Bihar, where communal polarization would help BJP electorally. At each stop, the procession staged rallies where communal rhetoric incited local tensions, often resulting in riots.
- **Kar seva (volunteer service)**: The VHP organized multiple kar sevas where "volunteers" would gather at Ayodhya, ostensibly to begin temple construction. These were rehearsals for the eventual destruction, testing police response and building participant confidence. Each kar seva that was prevented by police was then framed as state repression of Hindu faith, further mobilizing support.

The genius of the Rath Yatra was its ambiguity between peaceful procession and violent mobilization. Officially, it was religious pilgrimage. Actually, it was systematic production of communal violence. When riots occurred, BJP could disclaim responsibility while benefiting politically from polarization.

### December 6, 1992: Spectacle and Aftermath

The destruction of Babri Masjid on December 6, 1992 was not spontaneous but carefully orchestrated. Approximately 150,000 kar sevaks had gathered over preceding days. BJP leaders, including Advani and Murli Manohar Joshi, were present on a dais overlooking the mosque.

What occurred was demolition theater. Young men with hammers and pickaxes climbed the mosque and methodically destroyed it over several hours, with thousands watching and cheering. The entire event was filmed and photographed, producing a visual archive that circulated through video cassettes and later digital media.

The spectacle functioned simultaneously as:
- **Victory celebration**: The destruction enacted the mythology—Hindus reclaiming sacred space from Muslim occupation
- **Recruitment**: Young men who participated became deeply invested in Hindutva, their actions binding them to the ideology
- **Territorial production**: The demolition didn't just destroy a building but marked Ayodhya as reclaimed Hindu territory
- **Mobilizational template**: The success emboldened further violence, showing that organized Hindutva could overwhelm state authority

The immediate aftermath was communal riots killing approximately 2,000 people, mostly Muslims, in Mumbai, Bhopal, Ahmedabad, and other cities. But this violence, rather than discrediting the movement, consolidated it. For participants, the riots confirmed that Hindus and Muslims were indeed enemies requiring separation. For the BJP, polarization improved electoral prospects.

The long-term aftermath is even more revealing. The BJP was briefly banned, but electorally it surged. In the 1993 state elections, BJP formed governments in several states. The mythology had been validated through action—destroy the mosque, survive state repression, gain political power. The feedback loop was complete.

### The Legal Consecration of Mythology

The Ayodhya legal case continued for decades, finally resolved by the Supreme Court in November 2019. The judgment awarded the land to Hindus for temple construction while acknowledging that the mosque's destruction was illegal and that clear evidence of temple demolition was lacking.

This judgment reveals law's capitulation to mythology. The court's reasoning included statements about Hindu faith and belief being significant factors. In essence: we can't prove there was a temple, we acknowledge destroying the mosque was criminal, but we're giving the land to those who destroyed it because of their faith.

This legal validation completed the hyperreal circuit:
1. Mythology asserts sacred site
2. Mobilization destroys mosque based on mythology
3. Violence confirms mythology through action
4. Law ratifies mythology despite acknowledging its empirical weakness
5. Mythology becomes official reality regardless of historical fact

This is self-propagation at systemic level. The mythology generated violence, the violence generated political pressure, the pressure influenced legal judgment, the judgment validated the mythology. The system produced the conditions for its own confirmation.

## VI. Gujarat 2002: The Crystallization of Genocidal Governance

### The Production of Pogrom

To understand Gujarat 2002, we must reject the framing of "communal riots"—a term suggesting spontaneous, two-sided violence. What occurred was systematic, state-enabled targeting of Muslim civilians. The Concerned Citizens Tribunal documented extensive evidence of:
- Police ordered to stand down or actively participate in attacks
- Lists of Muslim homes and businesses prepared in advance
- Systematic sexual violence against Muslim women
- Commercial property destroyed to eliminate Muslim economic position
- Specific targeting of Muslim professionals and community leaders

The triggering incident—the Godhra train burning on February 27, 2002, which killed 59 Hindu pilgrims returning from Ayodhya—remains disputed. Was it accidental fire? Muslim mob attack? Deliberately provoked incident? The uncertainty matters because it shows how mythology works: before facts were established, the VHP had declared it "Islamic terrorism" and called for revenge.

The violence that followed was concentrated in February 28-March 2. Approximately 1,000-2,000 Muslims were killed (official count was minimized; actual deaths likely higher). Sexual violence was especially horrific—pregnant women were cut open, mass rapes were public spectacles meant to humiliate entire communities.

But the violence wasn't just destructive; it was productive. It produced:
- **Spatial segregation**: Neighborhoods became definitively Hindu or Muslim through violence and displacement. Mixed communities were impossible after relatives were massacred. This segregation persists 20+ years later.
- **Economic subordination**: Muslim-owned businesses, shops, and factories were systematically destroyed, eliminating middle-class economic position and forcing dependence.
- **Political consolidation**: Modi emerged from the pogrom politically strengthened, winning state elections in December 2002. The violence demonstrated his willingness to enable Hindu supremacy, which his core constituency rewarded electorally.
- **Subjective transformation**: Both perpetrators and silent bystanders were transformed. Perpetrators became invested in justifying their actions, requiring acceptance of the mythology that legitimated violence. Bystanders who didn't resist became complicit, their silence purchased through fear or approval.

### Modi's Role: Complicity and Calculation

Narendra Modi was Chief Minister during the pogrom. His role has been debated extensively, but key evidence points to at minimum passive enablement:
- Cabinet minister Haren Pandya (later murdered) testified that Modi told police not to interfere with "Hindu anger"
- Senior police officers have stated they received no orders to stop violence
- Modi appeared on TV calling the violence "reaction" to Godhra, legitimating it
- State machinery actively hindered rescue and relief efforts for Muslims
- Subsequent investigations into the violence were systematically obstructed

Whether Modi directly ordered violence or simply allowed it to occur is almost irrelevant. As Chief Minister, he had capacity to stop it and chose not to. This choice transformed him from politician to Hindutva icon—the leader willing to enable violence against Muslims.

The 2002 violence should have ended Modi's career. Instead, it launched his national trajectory. This reveals something crucial about how Hindutva mythology operates: violence is not obstacle to power but pathway to power. Demonstrating willingness to harm Muslims proves commitment to Hindu cause.

### The Gujarat Model: Neoliberalism and Hindutva as Synthesis

Post-2002, Modi pivoted to development rhetoric, promoting Gujarat as investment destination, building infrastructure, and courting domestic and international capital. This has been presented as transformation from communal politics to developmental governance.

But this is false dichotomy. Modi's Gujarat synthesized neoliberal economics and Hindutva politics in ways that reinforced each other:
- **Disciplined labor**: Hindu nationalist mobilization produced a workforce that could be presented to capital as disciplined, non-militant, and productive. Labor organizing was suppressed using Hindutva rhetoric of national development.
- **Spatial cleansing**: Segregation of Muslims into ghettos enabled "development" of Hindu areas while marginalizing Muslim neighborhoods, which could then be portrayed as backwards, justifying further exclusion.
- **Cultural nationalism**: Hindu pride narratives complemented make-in-India economic nationalism, producing affective investment in capital accumulation as Hindu achievement.
- **Authoritarian efficiency**: Hindutva majoritarianism enabled suppression of dissent—environmental protests, labor organizing, rights advocacy—in the name of development.

This synthesis is not unique to Modi but represents a broader pattern: neoliberal capitalism doesn't contradict fascistic nationalism but can be powerfully reinforced by it. Capital benefits from authoritarian state capacity, controlled labor, and nationalist consumption while accepting or endorsing cultural majoritarianism.

## VII. Digital Mythology: Self-Propagation Through Algorithmic Circulation

### WhatsApp as Distributed Production System

To understand contemporary Hindutva's self-propagating capacity, we must analyze WhatsApp's role. With 400+ million users in India, WhatsApp has become primary information source for millions, especially in smaller cities and towns where English-language media has limited penetration.

WhatsApp's affordances make it ideal for mythological production:
- **End-to-end encryption**: Content circulates without platform oversight, enabling extreme misinformation without moderation.
- **Group forwarding**: Messages can be forwarded to multiple groups simultaneously, creating viral spread that can reach millions within hours.
- **Peer-to-peer trust**: Messages come from family, friends, coworkers—trusted social connections rather than distant media institutions, lending credibility even to false content.
- **Multimedia format**: Videos and images are more emotionally affecting than text and harder to fact-check.
- **Lack of context**: Forwarded content loses original context, enabling videos from Pakistan or old incidents to be presented as current Indian events.

The BJP-RSS has weaponized these features systematically. During elections, coordinated messaging cascades through WhatsApp groups. Party supporters receive messages to forward, and within hours millions have seen the content. This doesn't require top-down control; once initial messages are distributed, the network amplifies organically through individual forwarding.

But the crucial dynamic is user participation. Unlike traditional media where audiences passively consume, WhatsApp requires active forwarding. Users aren't just receivers but distributors, making them complicit in mythological production. This participation creates investment—having forwarded content, users become psychologically committed to its truth.

### The Infrastructure of Manufactured Reality

The scale of disinformation is staggering. Research has documented thousands of false WhatsApp messages, including:
- Fake videos of Muslim violence against Hindus (often Pakistani footage recontextualized)
- False demographic claims (Muslims will soon be majority; Hindu population is declining)
- Conspiracy theories (love jihad; corona jihad; Muslims poisoning food)
- Fabricated quotes attributed to Muslim leaders advocating Hindu genocide
- Doctored images showing mosques where temples supposedly stood

Individual false claims can be debunked, but the system is resilient because participants aren't evaluating individual claims empirically. They're operating within a mythological framework where these claims feel true even if details are wrong. A fake video of Muslims celebrating Pakistan's cricket victory circulates not because viewers verified its authenticity but because it confirms existing beliefs about Muslim disloyalty.

This is hyperreality operationalized through technology. The simulation (videos, messages, images constructing Muslims as enemy) doesn't represent reality but creates its own reality that then shapes perception and action. People act on the basis of WhatsApp content—voting, spreading rumors, participating in violence—making the mythology productive of real effects regardless of its empirical basis.

### Algorithmic Amplification on Social Media

Beyond WhatsApp, platforms like YouTube, Facebook, and Twitter amplify Hindutva content through recommendation algorithms optimized for engagement. Content generating strong emotional reactions—fear, anger, outrage—gets more engagement and therefore more algorithmic promotion.

Hindutva content is engineered for engagement:
- Inflammatory thumbnails and headlines
- Fear-based messaging about demographic takeover
- Outrage-generating videos of alleged Muslim crimes
- Pride-based content celebrating Hindu civilization

Someone watching a yoga video gets recommended videos about "Islamic invasion of India." Someone searching for Ram temple information gets recommended videos claiming Taj Mahal was originally a Hindu temple. The algorithm creates radicalization pathways, moving users from mild cultural nationalism to extreme communal hatred through progressively more inflammatory content.

YouTube's recommendations have been especially documented. Researchers found that the platform's algorithm systematically recommends right-wing content more aggressively than left or center content, creating asymmetric radicalization. Someone can fall down a rabbit hole from innocuous Hindu cultural content to explicitly genocidal rhetoric within a few recommendations.

The BJP-RSS has built professional IT cells that produce massive content volume, game algorithmic systems through coordinated liking and sharing, and create fake accounts to amplify reach. But the crucial point is that this doesn't require conspiracy—the platforms' own design creates the conditions for mythological amplification.

## VIII. Institutional Capture and Mythological Statecraft

### Rewriting Education: Producing Hindutva Subjects From Childhood

The RSS's educational network, Vidya Bharati, operates approximately 12,000 schools reaching 3+ million students. These schools provide regular curriculum but supplement it with:
- Daily shakha-style assemblies with prayers to Bharat Mata
- History textbooks presenting Hindutva narratives as fact
- Valorization of Hindu kings and demonization of Muslim rulers
- Sanskrit instruction emphasizing it as sacred language
- Cultural programs celebrating Hindu festivals and ignoring or minimizing others

Beyond Vidya Bharati schools, the BJP has systematically rewritten state textbooks where it controls governments. The changes reveal mythology production:
- Claiming ancient India had nuclear weapons and airplanes based on mythological texts
- Portraying Muslim rule as uniformly oppressive while erasing syncretism and cultural exchange
- Minimizing or justifying caste oppression
- Promoting Vedic science as superior to modern science
- Describing India as Hindu civilization that periodically needs to reassert itself against foreign invasions

These aren't random errors but systematic reconstruction of history to align with Hindutva mythology. Students educated through these textbooks emerge with mythological framework already internalized—Muslims are historical enemies, Hindu civilization was glorious until invaded, contemporary Hindu unity is reassertion of eternal truth.

The long-term effect is producing generations who can't distinguish history from mythology because they were never taught the distinction. This is not ignorance but alternative epistemology—one in which mythological truth is superior to empirical fact.

### Academic Persecution and Epistemic Violence

Simultaneously, critical scholars have been systematically targeted:
- Removal from academic positions (Romila Thapar, D.N. Jha, and others pressured)
- Defamation campaigns and online harassment
- Legal charges and threats
- Institutional marginalization through appointment of RSS-affiliated administrators

This isn't simply political disagreement but attempt to eliminate alternative knowledge production. If historians who challenge Hindutva narratives are silenced, mythology becomes sole available framework.

The pattern extends beyond history to any field producing knowledge that contradicts mythology:
- Climate scientists facing pressure for contradicting development narratives
- Sociologists studying caste violence harassed as anti-national
- Journalists reporting on human rights abuses prosecuted under sedition laws
- NGOs documenting atrocities having their licenses revoked

The goal is comprehensive capture of knowledge production, making mythology not just one perspective among many but the only permissible truth.

## IX. Conclusion: The Dynamics of Self-Perpetuating Mythology

### The Circular Structure of Validation

Hindutva mythology self-propagates through circular validation across multiple levels:
- **Institutional**: RSS trains cadre → cadre captures institutions → institutions reproduce mythology → mythology legitimates RSS
- **Affective**: Mythology generates fear of Muslims → fear justifies violence → violence confirms Muslim threat → mythology strengthened
- **Political**: Polarization wins elections → power enables more polarization → polarization wins more elections
- **Epistemic**: Mythology rewrites history → rewritten history taught in schools → students educated in mythology → mythology becomes common sense
- **Technological**: Algorithms amplify inflammatory content → content radicalizes users → radicalized users produce more content → algorithms amplify further

These cycles reinforce each other. Educational capture produces subjects receptive to WhatsApp mythology, which generates electoral success, which enables further educational capture. No single intervention can break this system because it operates simultaneously across domains.

### The Caste Aporia as Permanent Instability

Despite this self-reinforcing structure, the fundamental contradiction remains: Hindutva requires caste hierarchy to function (providing organizational base, leadership, ideological content) but must disavow caste to achieve mass mobilization.

This produces permanent instability manifesting in three ways:
1. **Periodic caste revolt**: Dalit and OBC communities periodically reject Hindu unity rhetoric and assert autonomous political identity (BSP in UP, caste-based parties in Bihar, Dalit assertion movements across India)
2. **Intra-Hindu violence**: The mythology of unified Hinduism cannot prevent caste violence—honor killings, anti-Dalit atrocities, conflicts over temple entry, conflicts over land and resources
3. **Strategic incoherence**: BJP policy oscillates between appealing to OBCs through symbolic representation and serving upper-caste interests, creating contradictions that opposition can exploit

These instabilities don't automatically doom Hindutva but reveal its permanent incompletion. The unified Hindu rashtra can never be achieved because the social basis (caste division) contradicts the goal (Hindu unity). This impossibility is not weakness but source of perpetual motion—there are always new enemies, new threats, new mobilizations needed because the fundamental project is unrealizable.

### Mythology as Material Force

The essay's central argument has been that Hindutva mythology is not false consciousness or ideological distortion but productive force that generates real effects:
- **Real violence** (thousands killed in riots, pogroms, lynchings)
- **Real territorial transformation** (ghettoization, segregation, displacement)
- **Real political power** (electoral success, institutional capture, policy implementation)
- **Real subjective transformation** (production of Hindu nationalist subjects)
- **Real epistemological shifts** (mythology becoming official knowledge)

Understanding mythology as productive rather than merely representational reveals why empirical refutation is insufficient. Pointing out that ancient India didn't have nuclear weapons doesn't dislodge those who believe it because the belief does work—it produces pride, identity, civilizational confidence. Demonstrating that "love jihad" is statistical nonsense doesn't stop violence against interfaith couples because the mythology produces the violence that then confirms the mythology.

This is why the self-propagating nature of Hindutva is so concerning. The system doesn't require external validation; it generates its own through the effects it produces. Violence confirms threat, which justifies organization, which enables more violence. Electoral success proves Hindu awakening, which justifies more aggressive mobilization, which produces more electoral success.

The question is not whether Hindutva ideology is true but how it makes itself true through continuous production of subjects, territories, institutions, and violence that incarnate the mythology in material reality. This is mythology operating not as false representation of the world but as force transforming the world into its image.
