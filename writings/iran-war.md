## I. A Provocation Extended

In January 1991, Jean Baudrillard published the first of three short essays in *Libération* under a title designed to be taken as a scandal: *The Gulf War Will Not Take Place*. The scandal was not, as his detractors insisted, that Baudrillard was denying that people were dying in Iraq. He was not. The scandal was more precise and more disturbing: he was arguing that the event had been so thoroughly saturated by its own mediation that what Western audiences experienced was not a war but its simulacrum, and that this distinction was not trivially philosophical but materially consequential. The clean bomb-camera footage, the Green Room atmosphere of CNN's Baghdad bureau, the surgical-strike aesthetic: these did not merely represent a war, they *replaced* it, producing an entirely different object that followed entirely different logic.

The question this essay poses is whether Baudrillard's framework remains adequate to the 2026 Iran War, the joint US-Israeli "Operation Epic Fury" launched on 28 February, which has killed at least 1,900 people in Iran as of this writing, and whether, if it does not, what conceptual extensions it requires. The short answer is that Baudrillard was right but not far enough. The simulation has deepened structurally, the colonial dimensions he largely elided are now central, and generative AI has introduced a recursion that transforms the epistemics of the situation in ways his framework anticipated but could not fully theorize.

---

## II. What Baudrillard Actually Said

It is worth restating Baudrillard's core moves cleanly, since they are routinely caricatured. His argument has three interrelated prongs.

First, the *spectacle* argument: the Gulf War was produced for a Western consumer public that had lost faith in war after Vietnam. The clean footage, no bodies, no blood, precise coordinates on green screens, was not incidental propaganda but the war's fundamental mode of existence for Western audiences. The war was "asexual, surgical," in Baudrillard's phrasing; enemy combatants appeared only as blinking computerized targets. Iraqi deaths, which numbered in the hundreds of thousands, were effectively excluded from the representational order. The war "did not really take place" from the West's point of view precisely because the West's point of view was carefully engineered to make it not take place.

Second, the *deterrence* argument: by 1991, war had entered a crisis. The Cold War had been fought and won by economic, informational, and electronic means. What succeeded it was not the hot war that had been deterred but a new kind of operation, deterrence continued by other means, in which the aim was never direct engagement but the management of perception and the consolidation of hegemonic consensus. The Gulf War was not a war but the *performance* of a war, staged to demonstrate that the new world order could and would enforce itself.

Third, the *information hostage* argument: we are all hostages on the global media stage. The warrior has been displaced by the hostage as the principal figure of conflict. Information saturation produces not knowledge but aporia. The identity of events becomes undecidable because the informational object that circulates is always already distinct from the material event it purports to represent.

Each of these three arguments applies to the 2026 Iran War. Each must also be revised under the pressure of generative AI and the specific political economy of social media.

## III. Operation Epic Fury: The War That Arrived Pre-Named

Before proceeding to the epistemics of mediation, a note on colonial reality: at least 1,900 people have been killed in Iran since February 28, including more than 200 children, according to the Iranian foreign minister. The most jarring single attack was the bombing of a girls' school in Minab, which Iranian officials say killed more than 170 people, mostly children. These numbers exist, in Baudrillard's terms, on the wrong side of the representational order: contested, filtered through a state already accused of disinformation, arriving through a near-total information blackout.

Consider the naming of the war. Donald Trump labeled the conflict "the 12 day war" during his announcement of a ceasefire in June 2025, before the second and far larger war had even begun. The name entered common usage well before the conflict it named had taken its final shape. This is the precession of simulacra in its most literal, almost parodic form: the brand precedes the event, the name is assigned before the conflict it names is concluded, and both brand and name are produced by the party with the greatest interest in controlling the war's meaning. The 1991 Gulf War at least required television to mediate between event and image with a slight temporal lag. Operation Epic Fury arrived already branded, already narrativized, a product launched with a press release and a war name.

The first twelve hours saw nearly 900 strikes targeting Iranian missiles, air defenses, military infrastructure, and leadership. The scale is real and material. But the official justification, nuclear proliferation prevention, arrives carrying its own internal simulation. The Defense Intelligence Agency had concluded in 2025 that it would be a decade before Iran could develop the technical capacity to produce a meaningful intercontinental arsenal, and the Department of Defense estimated the June 2025 strikes had set back Iran's nuclear program by only two years. The casus belli was itself partly a simulation of the threat it claimed to be responding to. The war against a nuclear Iran that *might* take place replaced the war against a nuclear Iran that was not, in fact, imminently taking place. The simulacrum of the threat preceded and produced the war against it.

## IV. The Deepfake as the New Simulacrum, and Its Structural Asymmetry

In 1991, Baudrillard's simulacrum was still a *representation*: a CNN broadcast, a press conference, a bomb-camera video. What generative AI introduces is something qualitatively different: simulation without a referent, images that are not manipulations of a real event but productions of a non-event. The ontological status of the image changes. Baudrillard theorized the image that hides or distorts reality. The deepfake is an image that was never indexed to reality at all.

By 2025, an estimated 8,000,000 deepfakes were circulating online, a 1,500% increase from 2023. The 2026 Iran war represents the moment at which these technological and geopolitical trajectories converged with maximal force. AI deepfakes, video game footage misrepresented as warfare, and chatbot-generated falsehoods flooded the information environment from all sides.

But the deeper point is the structural asymmetry in who produces which kind of simulation and to what effect. Citizen Lab has documented a coordinated AI-enabled influence operation called PRISONBREAK, assessed with high confidence to be conducted by an agency of the Israeli government or a close contractor. The operation deployed AI-generated deepfake videos, including footage of the Evin Prison bombing that appeared on X within approximately one hour of the actual IDF airstrike, a timing that implies either foreknowledge or a pre-staged contingency pipeline. Israeli Minister of Foreign Affairs Gideon Sa'ar reposted the AI-manipulated video. This is simulation synchronized with kinetic reality: the fake image does not lag behind the real event, it accompanies it, near-simultaneous, as if the informational object and the bomb are components of the same weapons system.

On March 4, the White House released a video on its official X account merging real clips of Iran missile strikes with footage from the *Call of Duty* video game. Halfway through, a choppy voiceover says: "We're winning this fight." This is no longer even a simulation that presents itself as real. It is a confident, public declaration that the distinction between documentary footage and video game footage does not matter, that the state has moved beyond needing its simulations to pass as real. What we are witnessing is something that might be called *post-hyperreality*: not the replacement of the real by the image, but the open annexation of image-space by power, which no longer bothers to disguise the annexation.

Meanwhile, the near-total internet shutdown imposed on Iran since February 28, with connectivity at approximately 1% of ordinary levels, has severed Iranians from real-time information and cut off the communities most affected from participating in the evidentiary record of their own situation. The colonial structure here is precise: the aggressor's disinformation apparatus operates at full capacity while the victim's capacity to document its own suffering is actively suppressed. The simulation does not merely obscure the war. It administers a selective blackout on reality itself, one that corresponds neatly to the geopolitical hierarchy. What Baudrillard described as the Gulf War's exclusion of Iraqi bodies from the representational order is here accomplished not only by editorial decisions but by military-grade cyberattack.

## V. The Netanyahu Episode: The Self-Sealing Circuit

The Netanyahu death rumor is perhaps the most theoretically dense single episode of the war, and it deserves careful unpacking.

During the second week of the conflict, a video of Netanyahu speaking at a podium began circulating widely. Users claimed he appeared to have six fingers on one hand, a known artifact of generative AI image synthesis. The phrase "Netanyahu is dead" appeared 218,580 times between March 9 and 17 across X, Bluesky, Reddit, YouTube, Threads, and Facebook.

The detail about the six fingers is the crux. AI image generators are notoriously poor at rendering hands. They produce extra fingers, fused joints, anatomically impossible configurations. This failure mode has become so widely known that it functions as a folk heuristic for detecting synthetic content: too many fingers means fake. What happened to Netanyahu's video is that a real camera artifact, a trick of light that made part of his palm appear to be an extra finger, was read through this heuristic and diagnosed as AI hallucination. The real was misidentified as simulation because it mimicked, by accident, the characteristic failure mode of simulation.

This is the point at which Baudrillard's framework must be extended. He theorized the simulacrum replacing the real. He did not fully theorize the moment when the real becomes indistinguishable from its own simulation and can therefore be dismissed as such. This is what has been called the "liar's dividend": Iran's years-long disinformation machine has contaminated the information space so thoroughly that its legitimate victimhood claims, civilian casualties, destruction of civilian infrastructure, are now viewed with deep skepticism by international audiences. The regime's information architecture has paradoxically undermined the credibility it most needs at the moment it most needs it.

The liar's dividend is the dialectical endpoint of the simulacrum: if all images are potentially fake, then real images can be dismissed as fake. The simulation does not merely replace the real. It *immunizes against* the real, making it structurally inadmissible.

Netanyahu's response was itself a textbook exercise in the impossibility of re-entering the simulation from outside. He posted a video of himself ordering a coffee at a Jerusalem café, captioned "They say I'm what? Watch." In the clip, a voice tells him that people online are saying he is dead. He responds: "I'm dying for coffee... Do you want me to count my fingers?" The proof-of-life attempt, far from closing the epistemic circuit, reopened it. Users continued to parse every frame of the video for signs of AI manipulation. On March 16, the Tasnim News Agency published an article titled "New Video of Netanyahu Proves Fake," listing alleged tell-tale signs as evidence that the coffee shop video itself was generated by AI.

The circuit is self-sealing. The real cannot re-enter because the tools for testing its reality are themselves compromised. Researchers at the Atlantic Council's DFRLab tabulated more than 300 responses by Grok, X's integrated AI assistant, to a single fake AI-generated video of a bombed airport. The bot's responses contradicted each other, sometimes minute to minute: one instance stating the video "likely shows real damage," another stating it was "likely not authentic." AI is now the mediator of truth claims about AI-generated content. The epistemological regress is complete: we are using a system prone to hallucination to determine whether images are hallucinations.

There is, however, an asymmetry worth insisting on. The Netanyahu rumor was debunked within days by institutional fact-checkers, and Netanyahu is clearly alive and continuing to direct the war. The bombing of the Shajarah Tayyebeh girls' school in Minab killed at least 168 people, most of them children, yet the Trump administration initially blamed Iran for the strike without providing evidence and has stated it is "now investigating." The simulation does not distribute its doubt equally. Rumors about a Western leader's death can be resolved by the leader appearing on camera. The deaths of Iranian schoolchildren remain epistemically contested, their reality held hostage to an information environment in which both the aggressor's propaganda and the defender's propaganda have, between them, made verification nearly impossible. Death in the bombed school, in the burning oil facility, remains what it was in 1991: excluded from the representational order by design.

## VI. AI Targeting and the Collapse of the Distinction Between Simulation and Killing

Baudrillard described the Gulf War's bomb-camera footage as the war watching itself, the technology of simulation integrated into the weapons system. In 2026, this integration has reached its logical terminus. US Senate Democrats have written to Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth seeking information about the Minab school bombing, including clarification on any use of AI in target selection. If an AI targeting system selected a girls' school as a military target, the simulation does not obscure the killing. It commits it.

AI does not merely simulate the war: it may be producing the target lists from which the war is fought. The simulation becomes generative of material reality rather than merely representative of it. This is the moment at which Baudrillard's provocation needs to be reversed and taken seriously rather than playfully. The Iran War *is* taking place, and the simulation is among the mechanisms of its taking place. The hyperreal and the real are not in opposition here. The hyperreal is the operational infrastructure of colonial violence.

## VII. The Colonial Structure of the Real

The Baudrillardian tradition has been routinely criticized, by Norris, Eagleton, and others, for political quietism: if everything is simulation, resistance becomes as meaningless as domination. This criticism misses what is most politically useful in the framework, which is its analysis of *whose* reality gets to count as real. The Gulf War excluded Iraqi deaths from the representational order. The 2026 Iran war extends this exclusion through multiple technical and institutional mechanisms: the internet blackout denying Iranians the tools to document their own suffering; the liar's dividend by which Iran's genuine victimhood is contaminated by its own propaganda history; the AI deepfake infrastructure that floods the zone with fabricated Iranian strikes while Israeli-linked operations like PRISONBREAK produce coordinated synthetic content with official state reach; and a Western media apparatus that registers Israeli civilian deaths with names and faces while Iranian deaths remain a disputed statistical abstraction.

Frantz Fanon argued that colonialism is fundamentally a system of epistemological violence: the colonized's experience, testimony, and selfhood are systematically denied the status of valid knowledge. The information architecture of the 2026 war is a contemporary expression of this structure. The real death of Iranian children is rendered epistemically equivalent to the fake deaths produced by Iranian state disinformation. Both are "unverifiable," both must be "approached with caution." Meanwhile, the West's narrative retains its default legitimacy, its structural privilege as the Archimedean point from which everything else is measured.

Achille Mbembe's concept of *necropolitics* is useful here. Mbembe argues that sovereignty is defined not by the right to make live or let die, in Foucault's formulation, but by the right to determine who is exposed to death and who is not. The information blackout over Iran is a necropolitical instrument: it determines that the deaths occurring inside it will not register in the international order as deaths requiring accountability. Iranian children die in the dark, epistemically speaking, while the Western public is carefully managed through a representational order in which proportionality, legality, and grief are distributed along colonial lines. The simulation is not a distortion of this structure. It is this structure's contemporary operating system.

Trump administration officials have offered diverse, shifting explanations for starting the war: pre-empting Iranian retaliation, averting an imminent threat, destroying missile capabilities, preventing nuclear weapons, securing natural resources, achieving regime change. The multiplicity of justifications is not a confusion but a feature. When the casus belli is itself a simulacrum, a rotating set of post-hoc rationalizations for a decision made on other grounds, its instability is not a weakness to be exploited but evidence of the system's confidence in its own immunity. No single justification needs to be true because the representational order does not require the aggressor to establish truth, only to maintain narrative saturation.

## VIII. Conclusion: The Real That Refuses

There is one thing Baudrillard underestimated, perhaps because 1991 gave him insufficient evidence of it: the real's refusal to be entirely absorbed. The simulation is not omnipotent. The girls of Minab are dead regardless of whether their deaths are properly inscribed in the Western representational order. The Strait of Hormuz is genuinely blocked. Oil prices are real. The war has metastasized across the region, with strikes across nine countries and an absence of diplomatic off-ramps that analysts have described as a structural condition for indefinite attrition.

Baudrillard's mistake, or perhaps his provocation, was to remain entirely within the analysis of the representational order without asking who benefits from the simulacrum and who is crushed by the reality it obscures. The Iran War did not take place on X, in the White House's *Call of Duty* montage, or in the Western news cycle's management of proportionality and legitimate grief. It is, in every material sense, taking place in Tehran and Minab and along the Persian Gulf.

The task of critical theory is not to be impressed by the simulation. It is to insist, against its logic, on the reality it is designed to occlude. Baudrillard asked whether the real could be recovered. The better question, in 2026, is: who is being protected from having to ask it?
