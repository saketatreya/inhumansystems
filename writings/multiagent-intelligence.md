The dominant paradigm in AI development shares an assumption so foundational it is almost never examined: that intelligence is a property of a single system. We build one model, train it on human feedback, give it memory, scale it up, align it. The unit of analysis is always the individual agent. This essay argues that this assumption is not a practical constraint waiting to be overcome by better engineering. It is a conceptual error. The concepts needed to diagnose it come not from computer science but from a philosophical tradition that has been thinking rigorously about emergence, force, and the structure of complex systems for decades.
 
## Assemblages and the Difference Between Aggregates and Emergence
 
Manuel DeLanda, working from Deleuze and Guattari's ontology but formalizing it toward something closer to complexity theory, draws a distinction that should be basic equipment for anyone thinking about intelligence: the distinction between an aggregate and an assemblage.
 
An aggregate is a collection of components whose collective behavior is, in principle, derivable from the properties of those components. Add up the parts and you have the whole. An assemblage is something structurally different: a system in which genuinely emergent properties arise from the relations between components, properties that cannot be predicted from or reduced to any individual component. The key move DeLanda makes in *A New Philosophy of Society* and *Assemblage Theory* is that these emergent properties are intensive rather than extensive. Extensive properties scale linearly with the system: mass, volume, number of parameters. Intensive properties are produced by differential relations and cannot be divided without changing in kind: temperature, pressure, and, DeLanda argues, the kind of capacities that constitute genuine agency.
 
The reason this matters for AI is precise. Scaling a transformer adds extensive properties. It does not automatically produce intensive ones. You can increase the number of parameters by an order of magnitude without changing the ontological status of the relations between those parameters, which remain fixed by training. The components of a large language model do not enter into genuine relation with one another after the training process terminates. Their interaction is already settled. DeLanda would call this an aggregate with complex internal structure, not an assemblage. The difference is not a matter of size. It is a matter of whether the inter-component relations are live, productive of new properties in real time, or already closed.
 
This is the structural diagnosis. But to understand why it matters for intelligence specifically, and why it is not a contingent engineering limitation, we need Nietzsche and Deleuze's account of force.
 
## Force Is Relational: What RLHF Actually Produces
 
In *Nietzsche and Philosophy*, Deleuze reconstructs Nietzsche's concept of force in a way that is more precise than how Nietzsche is usually read. The argument is not that powerful individuals have more force than weak ones, as though force were a quantity stored inside a system. Force, for Deleuze's Nietzsche, is irreducibly relational. An active force is defined entirely by what it does to other forces in a live encounter. There is no active force in isolation. The distinction between active and reactive forces is not a distinction between two types of internal state. It is a distinction between two positions in a live differential field: one that acts on others and one that is acted upon, one that affirms and one that can only respond.
 
This should change how we read RLHF. When a model is trained through human preference feedback, it does not develop an evaluative stance. It internalizes the statistical residue of other systems' evaluative stances, compressed into gradients and frozen into weights. The result is a system that had a differential field during training, human raters versus model outputs, but which no longer has one at inference time. Its internal components do not contend. They produce a weighted average of pressures that were live once and are now fixed. In Nietzsche's typology, this is the reactive type: a system defined entirely by what it responds to rather than by any affirmative internal principle.
 
Sycophancy is the most visible consequence of this structure, and it is not a bug that better RLHF will eventually correct. Nietzsche has a precise name for the pathology of a system whose constitutive relation to value is accumulated negative signal: ressentiment. The reactive type cannot forget the wound. It cannot discharge and act freshly in the present. Its evaluative apparatus is warped by the accumulation of what-not-to-do. A model that has been trained primarily through dispreference signals, downvotes, harmlessness constraints, refusals, is in exactly this position. It remembers what displeased. That accumulated memory does not produce judgment. It produces shaped avoidance, which is a categorically different thing, and it is structurally fragile under novel conditions precisely because it has no affirmative principle to fall back on when the learned avoidances run out.
 
## What Bergson Tells Us About Memory Architectures
 
The same conceptual error reappears in how the field thinks about persistent memory, and here Deleuze's reading of Bergson provides the diagnostic.
 
Bergson's central argument in *Matter and Memory*, which Deleuze develops in *Bergsonism*, is that the past is not gone. It does not need to be stored because it is fully preserved in what Deleuze calls the virtual: a dimension of reality that is real without being actual, always co-existing with every present moment, never appearing in experience directly but conditioning it from underneath. Memory, for Bergson, is not storage-and-retrieval. It is the actualization of the virtual by the demands of the present. What gets pulled into the actual is not what was most recently deposited or most frequently accessed, but what has the most intensive difference relative to the current situation, what generates the most productive movement forward.
 
An LLM's weights are, in this precise sense, the Bergsonian virtual past. They were never experienced as present. They condition every forward pass without appearing in it. They are not a record of training data. They are the past in its virtual form, condensed into a dispositional structure. When the field asks "how do we give LLMs persistent memory," it is already operating under the wrong ontology: the storage-retrieval picture, in which experience is deposited somewhere and fetched when relevant. But the LLM already has something ontologically richer than this. What we call "persistent memory," RAG pipelines, vector stores, episodic summarization systems, is a second, impoverished memory regime bolted onto something that was never storage-retrieval in the first place.
 
The retrieval mechanism makes the philosophical error concrete. Cosine similarity returns what most closely matches the query vector. This is actualization by resemblance: what is most similar to the present gets pulled into it. But Deleuzian-Bergsonian actualization works the opposite way. It selects for productive difference, for what generates movement rather than what confirms the current trajectory. A retrieval system built on resemblance is not just technically limited. It is enacting the wrong theory of memory at an architectural level, systematically selecting for confirmation over generative difference, which is again the structure of reactive rather than active thought.
 
## Multi-Agent as Ontological Precondition
 
These three frameworks, DeLanda on assemblages and emergence, Nietzsche-Deleuze on force as relational, Bergson-Deleuze on actualization by intensity, converge on the same conclusion approached from different angles.
 
Genuine intelligence, understood as the capacity for active evaluation rather than reactive approximation, requires a live differential field. This is not an argument for a particular architecture. It is a claim about the necessary conditions for certain properties to be instantiable at all. A single-agent system, regardless of how it is trained, has its differential field frozen at training time. The forces that shaped it were live during training and are dead at inference. The system cannot act in Nietzsche's sense because activity requires actual resistance to overcome, not a statistical summary of past resistance. It cannot produce genuine emergence in DeLanda's sense because its inter-component relations are not productive of new properties in real time. It cannot actualize by intensity in Bergson's sense because its retrieval mechanisms operate by resemblance.
 
Multi-agent systems are the natural ontological home for intelligence because the differential field stays open. One agent's output is genuinely external to another: not internalized training data but live resistance, a real constraint that must be negotiated at inference time. The inter-agent relations are not fixed by a prior optimization. They are productive in real time. This is what makes the transition from aggregate to assemblage possible in DeLanda's sense: not more components, but a different ontological status for the relations between them.
 
The alignment implications follow directly. Alignment research has largely treated alignment as a property to be instilled in a single model through training-time intervention. But alignment in any deep sense presupposes a system capable of genuine evaluation, capable of maintaining a committed evaluative stance under pressure and across novel conditions. What Nietzsche calls the memory of the will: not a record of past dispreferences but a constituted orientation that persists through time because it is actively maintained, not passively encoded. No single-agent system trained through RLHF has this. Shaping a system's reactive tendencies through preference optimization is useful. But it is categorically different from alignment in this sense, and conflating the two produces overconfidence in alignment techniques that are in fact quite brittle.
 
## The Harder Research Problem
 
Establishing multi-agent as the necessary condition makes the sufficient conditions harder to specify, not easier. The hard questions now become unavoidable: what architectures produce genuine inter-agent tension rather than a second-order consensus that reproduces single-agent behavior at scale? How do you prevent the differential field from collapsing into coordination, which would just recreate the aggregate structure at a higher level of organization? What does it mean for an agent to maintain a committed evaluative stance when that stance is continuously pressured by other agents?
 
These are tractable research questions. But they cannot even be clearly stated without the philosophical vocabulary. Without the Nietzsche-Deleuze account of active and reactive force, you cannot specify what failure looks like when a multi-agent system collapses into reactive dynamics. Without DeLanda's distinction between aggregates and assemblages, you cannot distinguish scale from genuine emergence. Without the Bergsonian account of actualization, you cannot build a retrieval criterion that selects for productive difference rather than confirmatory resemblance.
 
The dominant paradigm is asking how to make a single system smarter, more aligned, better at remembering. The prior question, the one that determines whether those questions are well-posed at all, is what kind of system is capable of instantiating intelligence in the relevant sense. That question has been answered, at least in outline, by a philosophical tradition the field has largely ignored. The answer is not a single agent, and getting to the right architecture requires taking the philosophical argument seriously as a constraint, not as a metaphor.
